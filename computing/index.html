Computing

With a unique combination of a strong background in astrophysics and a robust foundation in computer science, I have developed a diverse skill set that includes proficiency in several programming languages, including Python, Java, and Fortran. I have a keen understanding of machine learning principles and a deep familiarity with high-performance computing (HPC), including experience with GPU-accelerated workflows and parallel computing techniques.

In my research work, I utilize the FLASH code to simulate the mergers of white dwarf binaries, an application that not only showcases my computational abilities but also the depth of my understanding of astrophysical phenomena. I have further enhanced the performance of these simulations by applying warp-level programming and CUDA libraries on NVIDIA GPUs, leading to more efficient and larger scale simulations.

I execute these FLASH codes and post-process data on the Stampede2 supercomputer, the flagship of the Extreme Science and Engineering Discovery Environment (XSEDE). Post-processing involves the analysis and manipulation of the data produced by the simulations to extract meaningful insights and results. For this purpose, I employ the yt tool, a powerful open-source software package for analyzing and visualizing scientific data.

Additionally, I've streamlined workflows and enhanced operational efficiency by leveraging Linux command line tools and Git for version control and collaboration. This blend of computational and collaborative skills has proven instrumental in my work, and I'm excited to continue applying and expanding these skills in new computational challenges.

Outside of my professional endeavors, I am also a keen learner and enjoy tackling new projects in my free time. One such project is the development of a deep neural network (DNN) for classifying Type Ia supernovae using spectral data from the Berkeley Supernova Ia Program (BSNIP) and GAIA archive.
