Research

I am interested in understanding the fundamental physical processes that govern the formation and evolution of celestial objects. In recent years, the field of astrophysics has seen a number of significant developments, such as the discovery of exoplanets and the detection of gravitational waves, which have expanded our knowledge of the universe. Multimessenger astronomy has also played a crucial role in allowing us to study celestial events in unprecedented detail by combining data from multiple observatories and instruments. In my own research, I have focused on topics such as the evolution of compact objects such as white dwarfs and the role of magnetic fields in celestial events. Through my work, I aim to contribute to the ongoing effort to improve our understanding of the cosmos and to address fundamental questions about the nature of the universe.

I am building upon a novel Magnetohydrodynamics (MHD) solver (Bouchut et al, 2010) to get numerical solutions for White Dwarf (WD) mergers. MHD plays an important role in the long term evolution of these mergers and can lead us to better understand their progenitor systems. My current effort involves a direct comparison of the fully MHD solution of a WD merger versus that obtained for an alpha disk hydrodynamic treatment. Much of the previous literature (Shakura and Sunyaev, 1973) is still based upon the latter, in which the differential rotation in the tidally disrupted disk leads to a hot inflated envelope in the merger, which expands dramatically to non-degenerate stellar radii, in strong contrast to the MHD merger which remains compact. We are clarifying the thermal structure of fully magnetized white dwarf mergers , and comparing these to smoothly heated models given by the alpha disk prescription.

I am also interested in solving astrophysical problems using machine learning. I am developing and and training a Convolutional Neural Network on supernova spectra data from Berkeley SN Ia Program. The main objective is to reduce the supernova classification time from hours (template matching methods) to seconds without compromising on accuracy. I have also implemented segmentation methods that allow for better feature recognition.

I earned my bachelor's in Aerospace Engineering from UPES where my advisor was Dr Ugur Guven. My undergraduate thesis concerned the N-body simulations using Monte Carlo methods. I also worked on building orbital transfer algorithms (Hohmann) and modified them using RK4 scheme for interpolation. I created a machine learning algorithm that trained itself in real-time on data produced by the orbital analysis module which led to a ML based collision avoidance system. I also extended the solver to include effects like orbital decay, albedo and third body perturbations. I am particularly proud of my work because it went on to influence a bunch of other computer science students who then added elements of neural network in the solver and developed an orbital collision avoidance system.
