Research

My research focuses on stellar physics and stellar evolution. I create tools and insights that allow better understanding of formation and evolution of compact objects (white dwarfs, neutron stars, and stellar-mass black holes) and the various associated transient events (e.g., supernovae). There is a lot of new data coming in from missions like Kepler and TESS (with PLATO to come) which could reveal the properties of stellar interiors. Surveys like ZTF and ASAS-SN (with LSST on the horizon) will uncover fainter, faster, and rarer populations of transient phenomena. Gaia has measured the parallax, proper motion, and color of more than a billion stars. This astronomical amount data can also be efficiently and accurately handled using novel Deep learning techniques which vastly reduce computational time and resource. This is a very exciting time to be in the field of Stellar Astrophysics.

Mostly, I am developing Magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) solvers to build numerical solutions for SN Ia. MHD plays an important role in the long term evolution of white dwarf mergers and can lead us to better understand their progenitor systems. My current effort involves a direct comparison of the fully MHD solution of a white dwarf merger versus that obtained for an alpha disk hydrodynamic treatment. Much of the current literature is still based upon the latter, in which the differential rotation in the tidally disrupted disk leads to a hot inflated envelope in the merger, which expands dramatically to non-degenerate stellar radii, in strong contrast to the MHD merger which remains compact.

I am also interested in solving astrophysical problems using machine learning. I am developing and and training a Convolutional Neural Network on supernova spectra data from Berkeley SN Ia Program. The main objective is to reduce the supernova classification time from hours (template matching methods) to seconds without compromising on accuracy. I have also implemented segmentation methods that allow for better feature recognition.

I earned my bachelor's in Aerospace Engineering from UPES where my advisor was Dr Ugur Guven. My undergraduate thesis concerned the orbital analysis of trajectories of objects under influence of a gravitational source. I built on transfer algorithms like Hohmann and modified them using RK4 scheme for interpolation. I created a machine learning algorithm that trained itself in real-time on data produced by the orbital analysis module which led to a ML based collision avoidance system. I also extended the solver to include effects like orbital decay, albedo and third body perturbations. I am particularly proud of my work because it went on to influence a bunch of other computer science students who then added elements of neural network in the solver and developed an orbital collision avoidance system.
